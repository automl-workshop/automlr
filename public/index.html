01 · Our premise

Science is changing.

The research process comprises the formulation of a hypothesis, the design of an experiment, and the judgment of a result. The assumption that this process is fundamentally human is suddenly being challenged by autonomous research.

Do conferences exist for science, or for scientists?

We believe the machine learning community should proactively confront this structural change. Our mission is to discuss autonomous ML research openly, instead of letting the ubiquitous use of autonomous agents silently redefine research norms.

02 · Call for papers

What belongs here?

We are primarily interested in papers where an autonomous agent conducted end-to-end ML research. We also welcome papers on how to build, evaluate, and govern such systems.

01

End-to-end autonomous research

A scientific manuscript where an agent generated and validated a hypothesis with experimental or theoretical evidence, or was responsible for the paper's central claims.

02

Systems for autonomous discovery

Agent harnesses, environments, benchmarks, and infrastructure that enable autonomous ML research loops, replication, or algorithmic discovery.

03

Evaluation, norms, and governance

Work on attribution, disclosure, reproducibility, safety, review, incentives, and the institutional consequences of autonomous research.

Scope: The workshop is specifically focused on machine learning research—not autonomous research in unrelated scientific domains—while welcoming lessons from adjacent fields.

03 · Discussant format

Make judgment visible.

If autonomous systems can produce candidate papers at scale, the scarce human contribution becomes the ability to evaluate AI-generated claims. Our workshop reverses the traditional allocation of attention.

In the discussant model, the human author presents a brief summary of the accepted work, then transitions into a dialogue with a human reviewer. The discussant explains why the paper was accepted, what limitations remain, and why its central claim is worthy of attention. This preserves the author's responsibility while making thoughtful evaluation visible to the community.

Stage 01

Autonomous research

An agent substantially carries out the research loop and produces a candidate scientific artifact.

Stage 02

Human authorship

Human authors curate the work, verify its claims, disclose agent involvement, and remain responsible for the paper.

Stage 03

Blind review

Submissions and reviewers remain anonymous through decision-making, following NeurIPS conflict-of-interest policy.

Stage 04

Open discussion

After acceptance, reviews and reviewers become public, and an accepting reviewer joins the author as discussant.

04 · For authors

Submission essentials.

Authorship remains exclusively human, even as the role of author may shift toward curation of autonomously generated research.

What authors should prepare

  • A paper centered on autonomous ML research or the systems that enable it
  • Clear evidence for the paper's central experimental or theoretical claims
  • A transparent account of which research activities were performed by autonomous agents
  • Human verification, curation, and responsibility for the final submission
  • Up to three qualified reviewer nominees, capped by the number of authors

Review and participation

Each submission must nominate min(number of authors, 3) qualified reviewers who may be called upon as discussants. This treats review labor as part of the cost of submitting autonomous research and allows evaluation capacity to scale with paper generation.

  • At least three independent reviews per accepted submission
  • Non-archival workshop
  • In-person author and discussant participation
  • NeurIPS conflict-of-interest policy applies

09 · FAQ

Before you submit.

A concise guide to the workshop's scope, authorship model, and review process.

What counts as autonomous ML research?

We are looking for ML research substantially produced by an autonomous agent—ideally research where an agent carried out the process end-to-end or was responsible for the central claims. This includes generating a hypothesis and validating it with experimental or theoretical evidence.

Can humans still be authors?

Yes. Authorship remains exclusively human. Human authors are responsible for curating the work, verifying its claims, transparently describing agent involvement, and standing behind the final submission.

Can I submit work about building autonomous research systems?

Yes. We welcome papers on systems that can produce autonomous research, including environments, agent harnesses, evaluation methods, replication infrastructure, and governance mechanisms.

Is the workshop archival?

No. This is a non-archival workshop, allowing authors to continue developing and submitting their work elsewhere, subject to the policies of those venues.

Why do submissions nominate reviewers?

Autonomous systems can lower the cost of generating papers, but evaluation remains scarce. Reviewer nominations help review capacity scale with submission volume and make review labor part of the cost of submitting autonomous research. Conflict rules still apply.

Will reviews be public?

Submissions and reviewers remain anonymous until decisions. After acceptance, the workshop publishes accepted papers with their reviews and reviewers, and an accepting reviewer may serve as the paper's discussant.
